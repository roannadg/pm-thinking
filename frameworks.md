# Teresa Huang — Coaching Frameworks: Master Reference
Consolidated from: Mable second round prep, Mable CPO prep, Teresa coaching session 21 April 2026
Use this before any interview. These are the frameworks that apply across all roles.

---

## 1. Approach to Product

Teresa's framing (use this as your default answer):

"My approach starts with validating that the problem I have identified is actually the right one — talking to customers, reviewing data, making sure the work will drive business value and align with what the organisation needs. Once I am confident in the problem, I run quick discovery and delivery cycles to validate my assumptions as I go rather than upfront in one big phase."

The three validation lenses (DFV):
- **Desirability** — do customers actually want this?
- **Feasibility** — can we build it credibly?
- **Viability** — does it make commercial sense?

The output of discovery is a decision, not a report. Timebox discovery into 2-week sprints and push work into delivery in parallel — do not wait for discovery to be "complete."

**Senior PM shortcut:** Know when you do NOT need customer validation. Established patterns (checkout cart, search bar, standard navigation) do not need research. Moving fast on settled problems is a strength, not laziness.

## 2. Commercial Thinking

Teresa's framing: commercial thinking is understanding the drivers behind making money for the business and setting the right OKRs to move those drivers.

Show the commercial logic chain — not just a metric, but the lever:
- Example: "If a customer takes on more than 2 products they are highly sticky — so my objective is to increase product uptakes to drive CLV by $X."
- Mable version: participant activations with richer preference data → better matches → more bookings → revenue for Mable + jobs for workers.
- Buildxact version: active supplier account managers → builders get live pricing → CAC reduced by 15%.
- Performio version: enterprise customers stickier than mid-market → reduce pro services cost → move upmarket viably.

**Teresa's three value buckets** — all resume bullets and stories should map to one:
1. Cost reduction — reduced CAC, lowered effort, cut rework
2. Improved growth — expanded market, grew revenue, opened new segments
3. Improved efficiency — reduced time-to-value, faster decisions, improved throughput

## 3. Discovery

Full arc example structure:
1. What the data showed (where the problem was)
2. What the interviews revealed (why it was happening)
3. How it changed the direction (what you did differently as a result)
4. What you shipped
5. How you measured it

Run discovery and delivery in parallel. Timebox discovery sprints. Do not do a full research phase before delivery starts.

**Best example:** Buildxact onboarding — data showed drop-off in trial, interviews revealed blank canvas anxiety, changed from flow redesign to reducing empty states. 35% improvement in time-to-value.

## 4. Impact

Teresa's framing: impact is how you measure your team's work — less about how many features shipped, more about what success looks like before you start and how you track it.

**Success metrics framework (Teresa, April 21 coaching):**
1. Internal historical data — what has this metric done before?
2. Future business goals — what does the company need it to do?
3. External industry benchmarks — what does good look like in the market?

Targets should be ambitious but iterative. Be ready to articulate why a number wasn't achieved and what the course correction looks like — not just the win.

## 5. Stakeholder Management and Managing Up

**Teresa's explicit warning: do not be a yes person.**

Do NOT frame stakeholder management as executives overruling your decisions. That signals you defer to authority, not that you lead.

Instead: anchor to goal alignment.
"This is an important request — here is how I would evaluate it against our current goal. If it moves the needle more than what we have planned, I would reprioritise."

The outcome metric is your anchor. When an exec pushes for something, the question is: does this move our agreed metric more than what it displaces? That is a question, not a challenge.

**Leadership and clarity:** your job is to give the team such a clear goal that they can make decisions independently without you being the bottleneck. If you are approving every call, you are not leading.

## 6. Cross-Functional Collaboration

Teresa wants two types of examples ready:

**Good collaboration example:**
Show how you bring the team along — giving context so they are focused on the customer and the goal, not just the ticket. The kickoff structure (problem statement, assumptions documented, DACI, written brief) is your evidence. Best example: Buildxact onboarding redesign — engineering in discovery, not just in delivery. Team could answer scope questions without coming to you.

**Challenging collaboration example:**
A difficult engineer or designer — how did you provide clarity without deferring or overriding? Best example: Performio platform team tech lead — introduced three questions at the start of every initiative, ran "why/so what" exercise. Deployment time from 10 hours to 5. Work became visible and valued.

**For engineering specifically:** make their life easier, not harder. Clear brief, explicit scope, problem statement before solution. Bring them into discovery, not just the handoff.

## 7. AI Use

Teresa: prepare examples of AI in both discovery and delivery — not just as a writing tool.

**Discovery:** AI to synthesise customer interviews, surface patterns across multiple conversations, compress time from insight to decision. Do the grouping yourself — AI misses nuance and over-indexes on frequency, not commercial importance.

**Delivery:** AI for requirements drafting, stakeholder communications, assumption identification. Only works if upstream thinking is solid. "If the problem is not clear before you prompt it, AI produces polished garbage — confident, well-written, completely wrong."

**The test:** could you defend this output in a room without the AI in front of you? If yes, it helped you work faster. If no, you have not done the thinking yet.

**Building with AI:** Teresa specifically mentioned building something together. The Performio AI workflow — structured discovery with ~10% of the customer base, synthesis of calls, requirements writing, stakeholder comms — is the strongest personal example.

**Advanced (subagents):** run parallel agents across multiple data sources simultaneously — transcripts, surveys, support tickets — to get research synthesis in under an hour instead of two days. The mental model shift: treating Claude as a team, not a tool.

**The full discovery flow with AI:**
1. AI extracts raw problems from each data source (transcripts, surveys, support tickets)
2. You cluster into themes — human judgement, not AI. AI groups by surface language and oversimplifies. Two complaints that sound similar often have different root causes and different fixes.
3. AI populates a feature signal matrix — maps your themes against data sources to show where signal is strong (appearing across interviews + surveys + tickets) versus weak (only one source). Cross-reference table: themes on one axis, data sources on the other.
4. You size commercially — frequency × impact × dissatisfaction. AI over-indexes on frequency, not commercial importance. That call is yours.
5. You prioritise — RICE with squad, Value vs Effort with leadership.

**One-liner for interviews:** "AI is good at the extraction. I'm good at the prioritisation. I use it to surface what's there, not to decide what matters."

## 8. Motivations (Why Product / Why This Company)

Teresa's framing: motivation questions assess three things:
1. Passion for the function — why product?
2. Genuine support for the company and its mission — why this company specifically?
3. Willingness to solve organisational gaps — especially for senior roles

For senior roles: answers should be culturally driven and focused on business value. Not "I love building products." Show you understand what this specific company is trying to do and why you are the right person to solve it.

**What drives you (values level):** "I'm driven by complex problems where I need to think across the whole system — the customer experience, the operational reality, the commercial model, and the risks — and work out where the real leverage point is. I need ownership from problem through to outcome."

## 9. Marketplace Dynamics

Teresa's supply-side framing:
Identify which side of the marketplace is the constraint, and solve for that first. Once you solve the supply constraint, you must maintain the service level of the supply side at the highest tier continuously. The flywheel only keeps working if supply quality stays high — it is not a one-time fix.

## 10. Platform PM Framework

Teresa (April 21 coaching, CommBank context):
- Focus on the ultimate business outcome and goal first — design the platform around that, not around available services
- Prioritise scalability — the solution must cater to multiple channels and future growth pillars
- Protect platform health: key metrics are uptime and response time
- Ask for all constraints upfront in a case study — do not make assumptions you could have avoided by asking
