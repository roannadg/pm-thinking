# Commercial Impact Storytelling

A framework for communicating product work in terms that land with commercial stakeholders — executives, hiring managers, investors, sales leadership.

The problem most PMs have is not that their work lacks impact. It's that they describe it in delivery language when the audience is listening for commercial language.

---

## The structure: Top and Tail

> **Lever → Metric (from X to Y) → What you did → Outcome**

**Top (open with commercial context):**
Name the lever you were pulling and the metric you were trying to move. Establish the stakes before you describe what you did. Most PMs do this last — or not at all.

**Middle (delivery):**
What you actually built, decided, or ran. Keep this tight. The audience doesn't need the full project history.

**Tail (close with outcome):**
Return to the metric. Give the movement — then name the business impact one level up: revenue, cost, retention, pipeline, or risk.

---

## Why this works

Executives and commercial stakeholders are listening for: what was broken, what you changed, and what it meant for the business. They are not listening for: your process, your framework, or your team structure.

Leading with the lever tells them immediately that you understood what you were trying to move. Closing with the outcome at a higher level shows you understood why it mattered.

---

## The numbers problem

Percentages without baselines sound evasive. "35% improvement" raises the question: 35% of what?

Two options:
1. **Give the baseline if you know it.** "From 8 days to under 2 days — a 35% improvement in time-to-value."
2. **Estimate honestly if you don't.** "We hadn't tracked this precisely at the time, but based on cohort data, roughly X builders were reaching that moment in week one." An honest estimate is more credible than a bare percentage.

Always give both: the percentage AND the absolute movement. The absolute number makes the scale real. The percentage makes it comparable.

---

## Example

**Delivery language (weak):**
"I redesigned the onboarding flow based on user research, worked with engineering and design, and shipped a new first-time user experience including SSO, templates, and guided walkthroughs."

**Commercial language (strong):**
"The lever was builder trial-to-paid conversion — new builders weren't reaching value before their trial ended. I ran discovery to find out why, redesigned onboarding around the first completed quote, and time-to-value dropped from over a week to under two days — a 35% improvement. At the company level, that improvement contributed to the CAC reduction that made the enterprise supplier tier commercially viable."

Same work. Different frame.

---

## Common failure modes

- **Describing activity instead of impact:** "I worked with stakeholders to align on the roadmap" says nothing about what changed.
- **Outcome without lever:** "We reduced churn by 12%" — why? What did you change?
- **Lever without outcome:** "I focused on improving supplier relevance" — so what happened?
- **Percentage without baseline:** "35% improvement" — from what?
- **Stopping at delivery:** "We shipped the feature in Q3" — and?

---

## When to use it

- Executive updates and roadmap reviews
- Stakeholder alignment on prioritisation
- Performance reviews and promotion cases
- Interview answers (especially "tell me about your most impactful work")
- Written product updates and strategy docs
